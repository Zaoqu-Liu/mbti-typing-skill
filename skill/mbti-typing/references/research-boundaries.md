## Practical Boundary

MBTI typing can be useful as a structured self-reflection language. It is not a medical diagnosis, not a hiring instrument, and not a validated way to determine a person's worth, competence, or future.

Use the strongest available stance:

- "Interview evidence suggests..."
- "This is consistent with..."
- "This is a working formulation..."
- "This claim is from 16Personalities, not official MBTI..."
- "This is a non-standard observation, not established MBTI theory..."

## Source-Backed Claims

### Official MBTI Use

The Myers-Briggs Company frames MBTI as a development and self-awareness tool. Ethical use requires voluntary participation, confidentiality, feedback from a qualified practitioner for formal administration, and the respondent as the final judge of type. Do not use type to make selection or exclusion decisions.

### Reliability And Whole-Type Instability

Official MBTI materials report stronger reliability at the scale/preference level than at the whole four-letter type level. This supports using dimensions and evidence patterns rather than treating the exact four-letter code as permanently fixed.

Operational implication:

- Be more cautious with exact whole-type certainty.
- Present adjacent alternatives.
- Explain why the final type beats the runner-up.

### Big Five Relationship

Academic work comparing MBTI with the Five-Factor Model has found meaningful correlations between MBTI preferences and Big Five traits, while also criticizing the idea that MBTI preferences form clean dichotomous categories.

Operational implication:

- Use Big Five as a cross-check, not as a replacement label.
- Watch especially for neuroticism-driven false positives: anxious or turbulent people can look more introverted or feeling than their default cognition suggests.

### Cognitive Functions And Grant-Style Stacks

Function-stack interpretation is useful for qualitative interviewing, but it has weaker empirical support than Big Five trait models. When using Te-Ni-Se-Fi or Fi-Ne-Si-Te, treat the stack as an interpretive model, not a measured neural fact.

Operational implication:

- Prefer process evidence over type slogans.
- Label non-standard function concepts.
- Do not assign precise function scores unless an actual instrument produced them.

### Forced-Choice And Ipsative Questions

Forced-choice questions reduce some social desirability and "everything sounds like me" problems, but ipsative scores have psychometric complications. Newer forced-choice methods can improve measurement with careful design, but ad hoc interview questions are not standardized tests.

Operational implication:

- Use forced choice to generate discriminating evidence.
- Ask follow-ups and counterexamples.
- Do not compute official scores from custom questions.

### Bayesian Updating

Bayesian reasoning and likelihood ratios are excellent for disciplined thinking: evidence should change odds, and contradictory evidence should matter. But the likelihood ratios in a custom MBTI interview are subjective unless calibrated on data.

Operational implication:

- Use Bayesian tables as transparent reasoning aids.
- Mark numeric posteriors as heuristic.
- Prefer descriptive confidence in final prose.

### A/T And 16Personalities

Assertive/Turbulent is associated with the 16Personalities model, not the official MBTI framework. It is closer to a stability/neuroticism lens than a cognitive-function axis.

Operational implication:

- Do not write "ENTJ-T" as if `-T` is official MBTI.
- Use A/T only as an auxiliary descriptor for rumination, confidence, criticism sensitivity, and emotional volatility.

## How To Use Sources In Reports

Use source links when the user asks for rigor or when making claims about validity.

Recommended wording:

- "MBTI is useful here as a structured interview language, but I am not treating it as a clinical or selection instrument."
- "The Big Five cross-check is included because MBTI preferences correlate with several FFM traits, while the dichotomous type categories are debated."
- "The forced-choice questions are interview prompts, not a standardized psychometric instrument."

Avoid:

- "Scientifically proven type."
- "World's most accurate."
- "The Bayesian result proves..."
- "The function stack score is 95."
